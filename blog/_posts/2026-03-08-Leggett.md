Sir Anthony James Leggett, Nobel laureate and longtime professor of condensed matter physics at U. Illinois, passed away a few days ago. I'm sure we will hear from others who are much more qualified to speak about Tony's contributions and legacy, starting with his students and longtime collaborators. Here I instead offer a view from the sidelines, which might by its distance say something to the breadth of his contributions in multiple areas of quantum physics.

The plural "fields" is key here. To the extent that my own career across multiple playgrounds for quantum physics makes any sense at all, it is in large part because of the connections that Tony pioneered and forged. That same quality also makes his work difficult to summarize. His Nobel citation was "for pioneering contributions to the theory of superconductors and superfluids", which is generally understood to be the [theory of liquid Helium-3](https://journals.aps.org/rmp/abstract/10.1103/RevModPhys.47.331) that he developed. Important as that work may be, this is one case where the Nobel citation for a scientist should not be understood to summarize their overall contribution. Indeed, while I have learned a tremendous amount from Tony, I have never studied liquid Helium or read any of his papers on the subject. I once heard Tony refer to the significance of his work in liquid He-3 as being that it provided an almost unique example of a comprehensive description of a strongly interacting quantum system. That takes us closer to the significance of his work, but maybe still doesn't fully capture it (a reflection, perhaps, of his characteristic modesty and care in not overstating his case). My attempt to summarize Tony's most important contribution would be that he clarified how macroscopic quantum coherence appears across a variety of places, which may look superficially very different. Beyond liquid Helium, those places include several of the experimental platforms that I have worked with.

My PhD work studied the physics of collections of ultracold dilute atoms, suspended in a vacuum chamber. The context for this work was the breakthrough (which won a [different Nobel prize](https://www.nobelprize.org/prizes/physics/2001/summary/)) in trapping and cooling techniques that let us create a sample of these atoms that was colder than anything we know of, billionths of a degree above absolute zero. At these temperatures, the individual quantum wavefunctions of the atoms strongly overlap and influence the material properties, allowing for exotic effects such as Bose-Einstein condensation. Although this experimental development came relatively late in Leggett's career, he became very interested in it, and played an important role[^1] in developing the theoretical understanding of these effects. He especially helped to develop our understanding of the "BEC-BCS crossover", which describes how a Bose-Einstein Condensate (BEC) and a phase of weakly bound (Cooper) pairs (BCS superconductor) can be understood as two extremes along a smooth continuum.

More recently, I've been involved in the attempt to build a quantum computer out of superconducting circuits. This is a totally different experimental system---I have swapped atoms floating in vacuum chambers, shimmering under lasers' glow, for electronic circuits at the bottom of a cryogenic chandelier of wires. Nonetheless, here too I follow in a path whose theoretical foundations were laid by Tony. The [2025 Nobel prize](https://www.nobelprize.org/prizes/physics/2025/summary/) recognized some of the key experimental work that showed that quantum coherence could be observed and controlled on a large scale in electronic circuits (under special conditions, such as very cold temperatures and isolation from their surroundings). This large-scale controllable coherence is precisely what allows us to use these circuits as the building blocks for a quantum computer. As the lauretes of this prize tell it, Leggett was a key influence in the pursuit of these effects. Indeed, his is the first citation in the [Nobel committee's technical explanation](https://www.nobelprize.org/uploads/2025/10/advanced-physicsprize2025.pdf) of the prize.

Tony's point of view on how these effects should be understood in a unified way is best developed in his textbook, *Quantum Liquids*. It is a fantastic and ideosyncratic work[^2] (and, unlike much of Tony's work, it is written at about the advanced undergraduate level). The term "quantum liquids," which is not in very common usage[^3], allows Tony to talk at once about a whole set of superficially different systems. He uses it to describe how physical systems such as superconducting metals, liquid Helium, and ultracold atomic gases all can be viewed as variations on a theme. Thanks to him, in my own *random walk* through these quantum liquids I have been able to build on a systematic understanding, rather than trying to approach each one in isolation.

Beyond his specific contributions, I also found Tony's style of thinking through a physics problem pretty awe-inspiring. I got to see repeatedly this during my time at Illinois, but especially when I took his special seminar on superconductivity. One can [look through the lecture notes](https://courses.physics.illinois.edu/phys598sc1/fa2011/) for the course to get a feeling for it. His explanations were, to me at least, extremely economic and terse, and relied heavily on intuition and arguments based on dimensionality and energy scales to get at the shape of a problem. At the same time, once he had the lay of the land he would analyze it extremely thoroughly and carefully, often worrying about assumptions that were typically treated as irrelevant [^4].

I will defer stories of his personality to others but always found him to be utterly without pretension or bombast, which is notable within the context of his subfield of theoretical condensed matter physics. He was also a great supporter of the physics department at Illinois. That is how I came to have a signed copy of *Quantum Liquids*. He donated it to the Physics Grad Student Association for an auction, and luckily for me no-one beat my shaky grad student bid (probably because most of the other people there were also grad students).

Farewell, Tony. We will be reading your papers for many years to come.
